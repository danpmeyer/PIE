What is Proto-Indo-European?

Proto-Indo-European (PIE) is the reconstructed ancestor of the Indo-European language family — a group that includes Sanskrit, Greek, Latin, Germanic, Celtic, Slavic, Baltic, Iranian, Armenian, Albanian, and more.

Spoken approximately 4,500–3,500 BCE, PIE left no written records; it is reconstructed through the comparative method, comparing regular correspondences across daughter languages.

Reconstructed forms are conventionally marked with an asterisk: *pṓds “foot.”

Laryngeal Theory
The Three Laryngeals
h₁, h₂, h₃ — lost consonants with lasting effects
▾
PIE had three “laryngeal” consonants (h₁, h₂, h₃) that disappeared in most daughter languages but left traces — coloring adjacent vowels and lengthening them when adjacent to *e.
h₂ colors *e → *a: *h₂ánti “in front” → Latin ante
h₃ colors *e → *o: *h₃rẖéƵs “king” → Latin rēx
Preserved in Hittite: *h₂ster- “star” appears as Hittite ḥasterz
