Laryngeal Theory
The Three Laryngeals
h₁, h₂, h₃ — lost consonants with lasting effects
▾
PIE had three “laryngeal” consonants that disappeared in most daughter languages but left traces — colouring adjacent vowels and lengthening them.
h₂ colours *e → *a: *h₂ánti “in front” → Latin ante
h₃ colours *e → *o: *h₃rẖéƵs “king” → Latin rēx
Preserved in Hittite: *h₂ster- “star” → Hittite ḥasterz

About

PIE Study is a spaced-repetition companion for reconstructed Proto-Indo-European, built around the principle that every root is best understood through the English words it explains.

Data compiled from Mallory & Adams, Fortson, and the LIV.

Proto-Indo-European
One language.
Two hundred and fifty generations.
Around 4000 BC, a single language was spoken from the Atlantic coast to Central Asia. Every time you say father, night, or three, you’re using a word that has been spoken for 6,000 years. This app teaches you that language.
How it works
⚡
Spaced repetition
Flashcards that schedule themselves. Roots you know well appear rarely; shakier ones come back sooner.
🔓
Five stages
From the most familiar roots (father, two, eye) through to specialist phonology. Each stage unlocks as you learn.
💡
English connections
Every root is explained through the English words it produced — some obvious, some surprising.

“foot” — 6,000 years ago
foot

pedestrian
Latin ped-
pedigree
crane’s foot
PIE *p became English f through Grimm’s Law — but Latin kept the original *p. That’s why foot and pedestrian look so different, yet come from the same word.
